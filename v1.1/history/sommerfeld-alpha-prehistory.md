## Before α: The Spectral Line Puzzle (1887–1913)

### Michelson & Morley's Fine Structure Discovery (1887)
Before α was a "fundamental constant," it was a **spectroscopic anomaly**. Michelson and Morley, using their interferometer (the same instrument that failed to detect the luminiferous aether), observed that the hydrogen Hα line (Balmer's n=3→2 transition at 656.3 nm) was not a single line but a close doublet. This "fine structure" of spectral lines was unexplained by the Bohr model.

The splitting Δλ/λ was approximately 1/50000 — a small but persistent deviation from the simple Bohr formula.

### The Balmer Series (1885)
Johann Balmer's empirical formula for hydrogen spectral lines:

$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{n^2}\right)$$

where R_H = 1.09678 × 10⁷ m⁻¹ is the Rydberg constant. Balmer's formula was purely empirical — no physical explanation existed for why it worked.

### Rydberg's Generalization (1888)
Johannes Rydberg generalized Balmer's formula:

$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right)$$

R = 109737 cm⁻¹ became the "Rydberg constant" — another reified proportionality that the α-π-Helix project would recognize as a geometric scale factor.

## The Bohr Model (1913): α Emerges

### Bohr's Quantization
Bohr's model quantized angular momentum: L = nħ. From this, the electron's orbital velocity in the ground state is:

$$v_1 = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar} = \alpha c$$

The ratio v₁/c appears naturally as the dimensionless coupling between electron and photon. Bohr did not name it — it was just a number that fell out of the algebra.

### The Bohr Radius
$$a_0 = \frac{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar^2}{m_e e^2} = \frac{\hbar}{\alpha m_e c}$$

The Bohr radius is the Compton wavelength divided by α — another geometric relationship that the α-π-Helix project identifies as a scaling factor in the vortex hierarchy.

## Sommerfeld's Innovation (1915–1916)

### The Relativistic Correction
Sommerfeld extended the Bohr model to include relativistic kinematics. For an electron in an elliptical orbit:

$$E_{n,k} = m_e c^2\left[\left(1 + \frac{\alpha^2}{(n - k + \sqrt{k^2 - \alpha^2})^2}\right)^{-1/2} - 1\right]$$

where n is the principal quantum number and k is the azimuthal quantum number.

Expanding in α²:

$$E_{n,j} \approx -\frac{m_e c^2 \alpha^2}{2n^2}\left[1 + \frac{\alpha^2}{n}\left(\frac{1}{j+1/2} - \frac{3}{4n}\right) + \cdots\right]$$

The fine-structure splitting ΔE ∝ α² is the first appearance of α as a physically meaningful parameter rather than just a number.

### The Naming of α
Sommerfeld introduced the notation α in his 1916 paper "Zur Quantentheorie der Spektrallinien":

$$\alpha = \frac{2\pi e^2}{hc} = \frac{1}{137}$$

He called it the "Feinstrukturkonstante" — fine-structure constant. This was the first time a dimensionless coupling constant was named and treated as a fundamental parameter of nature.

### The Value Puzzle
Sommerfeld computed α ≈ 1/137 from spectroscopic data. The near-integral value 1/137 spawned decades of numerological speculation. Eddington, Dirac, and others wondered whether α was *exactly* 1/137 — and if so, why 137?

The α-π-Helix project's answer: 137 is not "special." α = r_e/ƛ is a geometric aspect ratio that happens to be approximately 1/137 for the electron. Different particles would have different α values if their vortex geometries differ. The "mystery of 137" is itself a reification — treating a geometric proportion as if it were a magical number.

## Historical Analysis: The Reification of the Coupling Constant

### From Anomaly to Fundamental Constant: Four Steps
1. **1887:** α is an unexplained 1/50000 spectral anomaly
2. **1913:** α becomes v₁/c in the Bohr model — a dimensionless ratio
3. **1916:** Sommerfeld names α and gives it theoretical significance
4. **1940s:** QED makes α the universal coupling constant — the expansion parameter for all electromagnetic processes

### The Reification Parallel with π
| Aspect | π | α |
|---|---|---|
| Original form | Circumference/diameter ratio | Electron velocity/c ratio |
| Discovered as | Geometric proportion | Spectroscopic anomaly |
| Named | 1706 (Jones) | 1916 (Sommerfeld) |
| Reification mechanism | Symbol → concept → entity | Symbol → coupling → fundamental constant |
| Mystification | Transcendental, irrational | ~1/137, "why 137?" |

### The α-π-Helix Insight
Both π and α underwent the same historical arc: discovered as relationships between measurable quantities → named and symbolized → reified into independent "constants of nature" → mystified ("why this value?"). The α-π-Helix project's core claim is that this reification is a category error: both π and α are geometric proportions that describe the *shape* of something (a circle, a vortex), not fundamental numbers that describe the *essence* of something.

### Counter-Argument
The historical parallel has limits: α genuinely encodes physical information (the strength of electromagnetism) that π does not. α could, in principle, have a different value in a different universe; π is mathematically fixed. The reification critique is stronger for π than for α.
