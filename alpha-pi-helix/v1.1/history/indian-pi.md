# Indian Mathematics: Aryabhata, Madhava & the Infinite Series Approach to π

**Task ID:** APH-1.10 | **Gap:** G6 (Historical Depth) | **Date:** 2026-07-17

## Aryabhata (476–550 CE)

Aryabhata's Āryabhaṭīya (Gaṇitapāda, verse 10) gives: π ≈ 62,832/20,000 = 3.1416. He explicitly says "āsanna" (approximate) — he knew this was not exact.

## Madhava of Sangamagrama (c. 1350–1425 CE)

### The Kerala School
Madhava founded the Kerala school, which developed results anticipating European calculus by 250 years.

### Madhava's π Series
π/4 = 1 − 1/3 + 1/5 − 1/7 + … (Madhava-Leibniz series)

Recognizing slow convergence, Madhava developed correction terms:

π = √12 (1 − 1/(3·3) + 1/(5·3²) − 1/(7·3³) + …)

Using 21 terms, the Kerala mathematicians computed π to 10–11 decimal places.

### The Infinite Series Mindset
Madhava's approach reveals a fundamentally different ontology:
1. **π as the sum of an infinite process** — not approached by finite polygons
2. **Algorithmic infinity** — a mechanical procedure to arbitrary precision
3. **No Platonic reification** — the series IS the method, not a definition of an ideal object
4. **Pragmatic acceleration** — correction terms for efficient computation

## Analysis: The Reification Spectrum

| Tradition | Method | Precision | Ontological Stance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Babylonian | Procedural tables | π ≈ 3 to 3.125 | Proportion (embedded) |
| Egyptian | Geometric squaring | π ≈ 3.1605 | Proportion (embedded) |
| Chinese (Zu) | Polygon iteration | 7 decimal places | Semi-reified |
| Indian (Aryabhata) | Stated approximation | 3 decimal places | Explicitly approximate |
| Indian (Madhava) | Infinite series | 10+ decimal places | π as sum of infinite process |
| Greek (Archimedes) | Finitary exhaustion | 2–3 decimal places | Ratio bounded but unnameable |
| European (Jones) | Symbolic notation | — | Fully reified |

### Relevance
The Indian case demonstrates that reification of mathematical proportions into "fundamental constants" is a Western European symbolic-linguistic phenomenon. The Kerala school achieved deeper understanding of π (infinite series, correction terms) than Europeans would for 200 years, yet never named π or treated it as an independent entity.

### Limitations
[PHILOSOPHY] The distinction between "π as infinite sum" and "π as constant" may be incoherent. Madhava arguably DID treat the circle ratio as ontologically primary (a well-defined limit), even without naming it. The real difference may be symbolic notation rather than ontology.
