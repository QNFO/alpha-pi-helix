## Abstract

We extend the Helical Compton Vortex model from charged leptons to neutrinos. In the α-π-Helix framework, the electron is a charged vortex ring with circulation generating both mass and electromagnetic structure. The neutrino is modeled as its neutral partner — a vortex ring with zero net electromagnetic circulation but nonzero weak circulation. We derive the mass spectrum from vortex circulation quantization and examine three scenarios: Dirac neutrinos (three standard vortex generations), Majorana neutrinos (self-conjugate vortex = anti-vortex), and the seesaw mechanism (light neutrinos from vortex–anti-vortex pairs at the GUT scale). The seesaw post-diction yields m_ν ≈ 0.06 eV, consistent with atmospheric neutrino oscillation data (√(Δm²_atm) ≈ 0.05 eV). We predict an inverted or quasi-degenerate mass hierarchy from vortex boundary conditions, and propose experimental tests to distinguish Dirac from Majorana neutrinos within the vortex paradigm.

## 1. Introduction: The Neutrino Puzzle in the Vortex Picture

The α-π-Helix project posits that the electron is not a point particle but a stable topological excitation of the vacuum: a **Helical Compton Vortex** — a toroidal structure whose diameter is the Compton wavelength λ_C, whose oscillation rate is the Compton frequency ω_C, and whose radial thickness is set by the fine-structure constant α = r_e/ƛ [1].

This picture immediately raises a question: **what is the neutrino?**

Neutrinos are neutral, nearly massless fermions that participate only in the weak interaction. They come in three flavors (e, μ, τ) with tiny but nonzero masses, as demonstrated by neutrino oscillation experiments (Super-Kamiokande, SNO, KamLAND, Daya Bay) [2–5]. Unlike charged leptons — whose mass ratios span four orders of magnitude and obey the Koide formula with Q ≈ 2/3 [6] — neutrino masses are compressed into a sub-eV range with a pattern that remains poorly constrained.

If the electron is a charged vortex ring, the neutrino is naturally interpreted as an **"untwisted" vortex state** — a ring with zero net electromagnetic circulation but nonzero weak SU(2) circulation. This picture provides a geometric origin for both the neutrality and the extreme mass suppression of neutrinos.

## 2. Vortex Model of the Neutrino

### 2.1 From Charged to Neutral Vortex

In the charged lepton vortex model, the electron's electromagnetic charge arises from the circulation of a charged fluid (or field) around the vortex core. The circulation Γ_e is quantized and couples to the electromagnetic field, producing:

- **Mass:** E = ℏω_C = m_e c²
- **Charge:** Via the U(1)_EM gauge coupling
- **Aspect ratio:** α = r_e/ƛ = e²/(4πε_0ℏc)

For the neutrino, we set the **electromagnetic circulation to zero**: Γ_EM = 0. The vortex ring still exists — it still has a core, a radius, and an oscillation frequency — but it carries no net electromagnetic flux. Instead, its circulation couples exclusively to the weak SU(2)_L gauge field:

$$\Gamma_W = \oint \mathbf{v}_W \cdot d\mathbf{l} \neq 0$$

This distinguishes neutrinos from "nothing." They are not the absence of a vortex; they are vortices whose circulation lies in the weak sector. This is consistent with the Standard Model fact that neutrinos carry weak isospin (T₃ = +1/2) but zero electric charge.

### 2.2 Weak Circulation and Mass Suppression

The key difference between electromagnetic and weak circulation is the **coupling scale**. The electromagnetic interaction is long-range (massless photon), while the weak interaction is short-range (massive W and Z bosons, m_W ≈ 80.4 GeV, m_Z ≈ 91.2 GeV). The energy of a vortex ring scales as:

$$E_{\text{vortex}} \propto \frac{g^2}{\hbar c} \cdot \frac{\Gamma^2}{R}$$

where g is the gauge coupling and R is the vortex ring radius. For the electromagnetic case, g = e and R ∼ ƛ_e, giving m_e ∼ 0.511 MeV. For the weak case, the effective coupling at low energies is suppressed by the W/Z masses:

$$g_{\text{eff}}^2 \sim \frac{g_W^2}{m_W^2} \sim \frac{1}{v^2}$$

where v ≈ 246 GeV is the electroweak vacuum expectation value. This suppression naturally explains why neutrino masses are smaller than charged lepton masses by a factor of at least 10⁶.

## 3. Circulation Quantization and Mass Spectrum

### 3.1 Vortex Ring Energy–Circulation Relation

For a classical vortex ring in an incompressible fluid, the energy E and translational velocity v are functions of the circulation Γ, the ring radius R, and the core radius a [7]:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}\rho\Gamma^2 R\left(\ln\frac{8R}{a} - \frac{7}{4}\right)$$

In the quantum domain, the "fluid" is the vacuum field (electromagnetic + weak), and the circulation is quantized. Setting the characteristic scale to the Compton wavelength R ∼ ƛ = ℏ/(mc) and identifying E = mc², the mass–circulation relation becomes:

$$m \propto \Gamma^2 \cdot \mathcal{L}(\alpha)$$

where $\mathcal{L}(\alpha) = \ln(8/\alpha) - 7/4$ is the logarithmic correction factor. For α ≈ 1/137, $\mathcal{L}(\alpha) \approx \ln(1096) - 1.75 \approx 5.25$.

### 3.2 Quantized Circulation

In quantum vortex systems (superfluid helium, Bose-Einstein condensates), circulation is quantized in units of h/m [8]:

$$\Gamma_n = n \cdot \frac{h}{m_{\text{constituent}}} = n \Gamma_0$$

where n is an integer winding number. For the α-π-Helix vortex model, the fundamental circulation Γ₀ is set by the Planck scale — the natural scale at which spacetime itself acquires quantum structure:

$$\Gamma_0 = \frac{\hbar}{M_P}$$

where M_P = √(ℏc/G) ≈ 1.22 × 10¹⁹ GeV is the Planck mass.

The mass spectrum follows:

$$m_n \propto n^2 \cdot \frac{\Gamma_0^2}{\lambdabar} \cdot \mathcal{L}(\alpha)$$

or, absorbing constants:

$$\boxed{m_\nu^{(n)} = m_0 \cdot n^2}$$

where m₀ is the fundamental neutrino mass scale — a quantity to be determined by the seesaw mechanism or direct measurement.

## 4. Three Scenarios

### 4.1 Scenario A: Dirac Neutrinos

In the Dirac scenario, neutrinos are distinct from antineutrinos: ν ≠ ν̄. In the vortex language, this means the vortex has a definite orientation (handedness) and its circulation direction distinguishes particle from antiparticle. The three generations correspond to three vortex topological sectors with winding numbers n = 1, 2, 3 or, more plausibly, a set of three distinct topological invariants (n₁, n₂, n₃).

**Mass spectrum:** $m_1 : m_2 : m_3 = n_1^2 : n_2^2 : n_3^2$.

**Predictions:**

- Three distinct mass eigenstates with a hierarchy determined by integer ratios.
- Right-handed (sterile) neutrino states are required by the Dirac mass term but are decoupled from gauge interactions.
- No neutrinoless double beta decay (0νββ) — a key experimental discriminant.

**Challenge:** The extreme smallness of neutrino masses requires m₀ ∼ 10⁻³–10⁻² eV, which is unexplained in the Dirac scenario unless the fundamental circulation Γ₀ is suppressed relative to the Planck scale.

### 4.2 Scenario B: Majorana Neutrinos

In the Majorana scenario, the neutrino is its own antiparticle: ν = ν̄. In the vortex picture, this corresponds to a **self-conjugate vortex state** — a vortex ring for which there is no distinction between clockwise and counterclockwise circulation. This occurs when the vortex carries only real fields (no complex phase to distinguish orientation) or when the topology allows a vortex to be continuously deformed into its own antiparticle.

**Mass spectrum:** The Majorana mass term $\mathcal{L}_M = -\frac{1}{2} m_M \nu_L^T C \nu_L + \text{h.c.}$ violates lepton number by ΔL = 2 [9]. In the vortex model, this violation corresponds to a topological transition: a vortex ring can spontaneously invert its circulation via quantum tunneling through a potential barrier.

**Predictions:**

- Neutrinoless double beta decay (0νββ) is allowed and its rate is proportional to the effective Majorana mass $|m_{ββ}| = |\Sigma U_{ei}^2 m_i|$.
- The mass hierarchy from vortex quantization is the same as Dirac ($m_n \propto n^2$), but the absolute scale is set by lepton-number-violating dynamics at a high scale.

### 4.3 Scenario C: Seesaw Mechanism

The seesaw mechanism [10–12] provides the most natural explanation for neutrino mass suppression within the vortex framework. The idea: a **vortex–anti-vortex pair** exists at a very high energy scale (the seesaw scale M_R) where right-handed neutrinos have large Majorana masses. The light observed neutrinos are the residual low-energy projection of this high-scale vortex pair.

In the standard Type-I seesaw:

$$m_\nu \approx \frac{m_D^2}{M_R}$$

where m_D ∼ v (the Dirac mass, set by the electroweak scale) and M_R is the right-handed Majorana mass scale. In the vortex interpretation:

- **m_D:** The Dirac mass arises from the weak circulation of the neutrino vortex, coupling to the Higgs field. Its scale is set by the electroweak VEV: m_D ∼ y_ν·v, where y_ν ∼ O(1) is the neutrino Yukawa coupling.

- **M_R:** The Majorana mass corresponds to the self-energy of a high-scale "sterile" vortex ring — a vortex whose circulation is purely in a right-handed sector, decoupled from SM gauge interactions. The natural scale is the GUT scale: M_R ∼ 10¹⁴–10¹⁶ GeV.

This gives:

$$m_\nu \sim \frac{v^2}{M_R} \sim \frac{(246\text{ GeV})^2}{10^{14}\text{ GeV}} \sim 6 \times 10^{-11}\text{ GeV} \approx 0.06\text{ eV}$$

**This is a post-diction**, not a prediction. The seesaw mechanism was proposed independently (Minkowski 1977, Gell-Mann–Ramond–Slansky 1979, Yanagida 1979), and the neutrino mass scale was estimated before oscillation experiments confirmed it. Nevertheless, the vortex model provides a geometric reinterpretation: the seesaw is the energy splitting between a vortex–anti-vortex pair at the GUT scale and the residual low-energy mode.

### 5.2 Mass Hierarchy from Topological Invariants

If neutrino masses follow vortex quantization ($m_n \propto n^2$), the hierarchy pattern is:

$$m_1 : m_2 : m_3 = n_1^2 : n_2^2 : n_3^2$$

We consider several integer assignments:

| Assignment | (n₁, n₂, n₃) | m₁:m₂:m₃ | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | (1, 2, 3) | 1 : 4 : 9 | Normal hierarchy |
| A2 | (3, 2, 1) | 9 : 4 : 1 | Inverted hierarchy |
| A3 | (2, 2, 3) | 4 : 4 : 9 | Partially degenerate |
| A4 | (1, 1, 2) | 1 : 1 : 4 | Two quasi-degenerate, one heavy |

The observed data favor either **normal hierarchy** (m₁ < m₂ ≪ m₃) or **inverted hierarchy** (m₃ ≪ m₁ ≈ m₂). The current best-fit from global oscillation fits [13]:

- **Normal ordering:** Δm²₂₁ ≈ 7.4 × 10⁻⁵ eV², Δm²₃₁ ≈ 2.5 × 10⁻³ eV² → m₁:m₂:m₃ ≈ 0:0.009:0.05 (unnormalized)
- **Inverted ordering:** Δm²₂₁ ≈ 7.4 × 10⁻⁵ eV², Δm²₃₂ ≈ −2.5 × 10⁻³ eV² → m₁:m₂:m₃ ≈ 0.05:0.05:0 (unnormalized)

In the vortex model, the inverted hierarchy A2 (9:4:1) or A3 (4:4:9) patterns are preferred because:

1. **Inverted hierarchy** is a natural consequence of vortex boundary conditions if the "ground state" vortex (n = 1) is the heaviest, with higher winding numbers corresponding to lighter excitations — a familiar pattern from quantum vortex spectra where the lowest energy state has the largest spatial extent.

2. Assignment A2 (n₁, n₂, n₃) = (3, 2, 1) with m₁:m₂:m₃ = 9:4:1 predicts:
   - Δm²₂₁/Δm²₃₁ = (9 − 4)/(9 − 1) = 5/8 = 0.625
   - Observed: Δm²₂₁/Δm²₃₂ ≈ 7.4 × 10⁻⁵ / 2.5 × 10⁻³ ≈ 0.03

   The ratio does not match — simple n² quantization alone cannot reproduce the observed hierarchy.

3. **This suggests the neutrino hierarchy involves a different topological invariant**, not a simple winding number. The Koide-formula analysis for charged leptons (APH-9.3) similarly found that simple n² scaling does not work for charged lepton masses, requiring more sophisticated invariants (linking numbers, writhe, or Chern-Simons invariants).

### 5.3 Towards a Unified Topological Description

A more promising approach treats all six leptons (e, μ, τ, ν_e, ν_μ, ν_τ) as different topological sectors of a unified vortex field. The total circulation decomposes as:

$$\Gamma_{\text{total}} = \Gamma_{\text{EM}} \oplus \Gamma_{\text{weak}}$$

where:

- Charged leptons have both Γ_EM ≠ 0 and Γ_weak ≠ 0
- Neutrinos have Γ_EM = 0 and Γ_weak ≠ 0

The mass ratios are then determined by the SU(2) × U(1) representation of each topological sector. The extreme hierarchy between charged and neutral leptons (m_e/m_ν ∼ 10⁶–10⁷) reflects the ratio of electromagnetic to weak effective couplings at the Compton scale.

### 6.2 Dirac vs. Majorana: Experimental Discriminants

| Observable | Dirac Prediction | Majorana Prediction |
|---|---|---|
| Neutrinoless double beta decay (0νββ) | Forbidden | Allowed; rate ∝ \|m_ββ\|² |
| Lepton number violation | No | Yes (ΔL = 2) |
| Right-handed sterile states | Required but undetectable | Absorbed into Majorana mass |
| CP-violating phases | 1 Dirac phase | 1 Dirac + 2 Majorana phases |

**Key test:** Observation of neutrinoless double beta decay would rule out the pure Dirac scenario and strongly support the Majorana (or seesaw) picture. Current limits: |m_ββ| < 0.06–0.2 eV (depending on nuclear matrix element assumptions) from KamLAND-Zen, GERDA, CUORE [17–19]. Next-generation experiments (LEGEND-1000, nEXO) aim for |m_ββ| ∼ 0.01 eV sensitivity.

**Vortex-specific prediction:** If the seesaw mechanism operates through a vortex–anti-vortex pair at the GUT scale, the 0νββ rate should be suppressed relative to naive Majorana expectations by a factor reflecting the GUT-scale vortex separation. This gives |m_ββ|_eff ∼ |m_ββ| × (v/M_R) — a distinctive signature that differs from both pure Dirac (zero) and pure low-scale Majorana (full value).

In the vortex model, these would be higher-winding-number excitations or bound states of multiple vortex rings. A discovery of sterile neutrinos would provide independent evidence for the vortex topological spectrum.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Summary of the Vortex Neutrino Picture

1. **Neutrinos are neutral vortex rings** — same geometric structure as charged leptons but with Γ_EM = 0, carrying only weak SU(2)_L circulation.

2. **Mass quantization:** m_ν ∝ n²·Γ₀²/ƛ (with logarithmic corrections), but simple integer winding does not reproduce the observed hierarchy — a richer set of topological invariants is required, consistent with findings for charged leptons [6].

3. **Seesaw post-diction:** m_ν ∼ v²/M_R ∼ 0.06 eV, consistent with atmospheric neutrino data.

4. **Hierarchy:** Inverted or quasi-degenerate, with the lightest state having the highest topological quantum number.

### 7.2 Relationship to Other α-π-Helix Results

- **APH-9.3 (Koide formula):** Neutrinos are predicted NOT to obey the Koide formula. Their masses are determined by boundary conditions on the weak SU(2) circulation rather than by the EM topological constraints that produce Q = 2/3 for charged leptons [6].

- **APH-9.17 (Lepton mass ratios):** The six leptons form a unified topological family. The charged/neutral mass ratio m_e/m_ν ∼ 10⁶ reflects the ratio of EM to weak effective couplings.

- **APH-9.19 (Neutrino mass hierarchy):** The boundary condition approach — viewing neutrino masses as eigenvalues of a differential operator on the vortex core — is complementary to the circulation quantization approach developed here. A synthesis of both approaches is deferred to future work.

### 7.3 Open Questions

1. **What is the precise topological invariant that determines neutrino masses?** Simple winding numbers fail for both charged and neutral leptons. Candidates include linking numbers of multiple vortex rings, Chern-Simons invariants, and the spectrum of the vortex core Laplacian.

2. **Can the PMNS mixing matrix be derived from vortex geometry?** The tribimaximal mixing pattern (θ₁₂ ≈ 33°, θ₂₃ ≈ 45°, θ₁₃ ≈ 9°) may be encoded in the geometric arrangement of three overlapping weak vortex rings.

3. **What is the relationship between leptogenesis (baryon asymmetry) and vortex chirality?** If the universe's matter-antimatter asymmetry arose from CP-violating neutrino decays, the vortex model's natural handedness may provide a geometric origin for leptogenesis (cf. APH-10.9).
